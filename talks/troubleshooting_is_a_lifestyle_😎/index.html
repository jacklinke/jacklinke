1. Terminology

Some Terms

Problem solving: Broadly describes working through a real or conceptual problem to reach a feasible solution.

Problem solving and troubleshooting are related, with troubleshooting being a subset of problem solving.

Some Terms

Troubleshooting: Subset of problem solving for...

  • recognizing
  • locating
  • resolving

...a specific issue in hardware, software, or elsewhere,

The process starts by recognizing there's a problem. We don't fix what we can't identify.

3. Indicators and Resources

Everything has indicators – cars, houseplants, pets, and software all show signs when something isn't right.

"If your only tool is a hammer then every problem looks like a nail"

- Abraham Maslow
For just about every *noun* that exists in this world, there are indicators of the health, functionality, or status of that thing, and there are often ways to add new indicators to provide more context. Indicators help us go beyond basic assumptions and look deeper into problems.

Indicator Categories

  • Built-ins vs Extras
  • Alert vs Status
  • Qualitative vs Quantitative
  • Affirmative vs Negative
Talk about different kinds of indicators and how they give us more context when troubleshooting.
There are various ways of categorizing indicators.
  • Built-in indicators may come with a product from the manuafacturer, or be inherent in the thing itself. Extra indicators are things we add on to provide more context or information.
  • Alert indicators are things that call out that something is wrong or needs attention. Status indicators show the current state of something.
  • Quantitative indicators provide a measured value, such as a a temperature measurement. Qualitative indicators are more subjective, like feeling that a device is warmer than usual.
  • Affirmative indicators are things that show that something is present or working. Negative indicators show that something is not present or not working.
We will focus on the first one for now.

Indicators

  • Example: Car
    • Built-in: dials, gauges, dashboard lights
    • Extras: CAN bus tool, tire gauge, fuse tester

Indicators

  • Example: Houseplant
    • Built-in: leaf color, wilting, moisture of soil
    • Extras: moisture meter, temperature sensor
But it's not just technical objects and ideas that have indicators - living things can have indicators, too.

Indicators

  • Example: Lady Duchess
    • Built-in: level of cuddliness, rate of treat consumption
    • Extras: GPS collar which logs location, step count, and hours of sleep
Even with pets, we use indicators to monitor their well-being.
Like other complex systems that cannot communicate directly with us, we can use indicators to understand what's going on.

While it may be surprising, these same principles also apply to troubleshooting software and systems!

... but we often forget to consider all of the indicators available to us when troubleshooting difficult problems.
Use all the tools at your disposal – don't limit yourself to the most obvious indicators.

4. Breaking Down Problems

Big problems are just a series of smaller problems waiting to be solved.

Use the example of a radar system to show how breaking down problems into smaller parts can help you solve them.
Also mention that making your software more testable is a great way to ensure we can break down problems into smaller, more manageable pieces.

The power of isolating variables & narrowing scope

Allows you to focus on one thing at a time and reduce the number of variables you need to consider.

In any complex system, you can only hold so much in your head at once. By isolating variables, you can reduce the number of things you need to consider at any one time.

Strategies for Dissecting Complex Issues

Isolate the problem into smaller, testable pieces.

  • Idempotent functions and methods
  • Performing only one task per block of code
  • Makes it easier when asking for help
Encourage to read up on testability, making functions and methods idempotent and performing only one task, and the benefits of writing tests.

In electronics, it's like isolating a circuit to test each component individually.

5. Avoiding Tunnel Vision

It's easy to get fixated on one symptom or potential solution.

Step back and re-evaluate periodically.

We talked about narrowing the scope and reducing variables, but there's a counterpoint - tunnel vision can prevent us from solving the real issue.

Need to be able to identify when you've been looking at the same variable for too long without progress, zoom out, and reassess your assumptions. Was there something you missed before you began narrowing your scope?

Consider the possibility that...

  • you made incorrect assumptions
  • you missed an important symptom
  • the problem is not what you think it is
  • the problem is not where you think it is
  • there is more than one problem 😬

Consider other angles when you're stuck. This can help you shift gears and try new approaches.

Broad perspectives and alternative approaches

Consider consulting someone outside your field or reaching out for help when you're stuck.

Knowing when to seek assistance

  • You are hopelessly stuck & frustrated
  • Want a new perspectives
  • Seeking greater/different experience
  • A fresh pair of eyes

  • Another brain and set of eyes working on your problem means new ideas and concepts might form, generating new directions toward solving a problem.
  • The process of describing the problem to someone else can often help you view the problem from a different perspective - and sometimes leads you to a solution or to identify things you might have previously missed.

Knowing when to seek assistance

Asking for help is not a sign of failure—it's a strategy.

Asking for help is a skill in itself. Sometimes, despite all our efforts, we don't see the solution, and that's when external perspectives become invaluable.
Share how Howard Aiken reached out to Grace Hopper to solve the complex programming of the Harvard Mark I.

How to Effectively Ask for Help

  • Provide context: Explain the problem clearly.
  • Be specific: Share code snippets, error messages, and what you've tried.
  • Don’t assume the problem is obvious to others.
  • Be open to suggestions and feedback.
Example: Howard Aiken reaching out to Grace Hopper to solve complex programming challenges with the Harvard Mark I. This highlights the importance of seeking assistance from those with different perspectives.

7. Documenting The Process

Write it down! A troubleshooting log is your best friend.

Logging helps with current troubleshooting and future reference. Creating logs helps keep track of what was done, why, and what worked or didn't.

Writing Down Your Troubleshooting Process

Document each step:

  • What was the problem?
  • What did you try?
  • Why did you try that?
  • What did you expect to see?
  • What was the outcome?
  • What else might you try?
Documentating your troubleshooting can be important - especially if you have poor working memory. It not only helps in the moment, but provides valuable references for the future.
